What Freelancers and Contractors Actually Need on Their Website (And What's Just Clutter)

Most freelancer websites fail for the same reason. Here's what actually does the job — and what to cut.

C
Cezar M.
Web Developer · czerwin.net

I recently built a portfolio site for a contractor. Before we started, he sent me a list of everything he wanted on it — a testimonials carousel, a "years of experience" counter, a full services breakdown with icons, an Instagram feed, a mission statement, and a contact form with eight fields.

We launched with none of that.

Not because those things are wrong — but because none of them were doing the actual job. The job of a freelancer or contractor website is simple: make the right person feel confident enough to reach out. That's it.

Here's what actually does that job, and what doesn't.

What You Actually Need

Your work, shown clearly

Not a wall of logos. Not a "projects" section with vague titles. Actual evidence — a screenshot, a photo, a before/after, a number. One real project shown well is worth more than ten listed by name. If you're just starting out and don't have work to show yet, a short description of the kind of problem you solve is enough. Just be specific.

One clear way to contact you

Pick one. Email, Messenger, a phone number — whichever you actually check and respond to fast. If you have all three on the page, the visitor has to make a decision. Most won't. A contact form feels professional but adds friction. Unless you have a reason to filter inquiries, a direct link works better.

One credibility signal

Just one thing that answers "why should I trust this person?" It could be a client quote, a company you've worked with, a credential, or even just how long you've been doing this. You don't need a testimonials section with a carousel. You need one sentence from one real person.

What's Usually Clutter

The full services list Cut it

Most freelancers list every service they've ever offered because they're afraid of missing a lead. What it actually does is make you look unfocused. Lead with the thing you're best at. The right client will ask about the rest.

The "About Me" essay Trim it

Your background matters, but three paragraphs about your journey is too much for a first visit. Two to three sentences about who you are, what you do, and who you do it for is enough.

Embedded social media feeds Cut it

Your Instagram grid on your website means your website is now dependent on you posting regularly. If you haven't posted in two months, it shows. Skip it — link to your profile if you want, but don't embed it.

The counter widgets Cut it

"500+ clients served." "10 years of experience." These look lifted from a template and nobody believes them unless they're backed by something real. If your experience is genuine, show it — don't count it.

The Honest Version

When I sat down with the contractor I mentioned, we stripped the brief down to three things: a clear headline about what he does, four project photos with short descriptions, and one contact button. That was the site.

It loaded fast, looked clean on mobile, and the first inquiry came within the week.

✅ The pattern that works Clear headline → work samples → one contact method. In that order. Everything else is optional.

Most freelancers don't have a website problem. They have a clarity problem. The website just makes it visible.

⚡ Worth asking yourself If a stranger landed on your site right now with no context — could they tell in 5 seconds what you do and how to hire you? If the answer is no, that's the fix. Not a new section, not a better photo — just clearer structure.
